Section3.AInterpreting Graphs

¶

  • Identify the quadrants and coordinates of \((x,y)\) points in the Cartesian coordinate plane.
  • Identify coordinates of solutions to linear equations.
  • Identify \(x\) and \(y\)-intercepts graphically and algebraically.

Subsection3.A.1The Cartesian Coordinate Plane

To get an idea of the behavior of an equation we can make a picture that represents its solutions. A graph is simply a picture of the solutions to an equation. To graph an equation we use a Cartesian coordinate plane (named in honor of the 17th century philosopher, mathematician and scientist, René Descartes).

The plane is divided into four regions, called quadrants, by a horizontal number line (labelled the \(x\)-axis on the graph below) and a vertical number line (labelled the \(y\)-axis).

The quadrants are labeled \(Q1\text{,}\) \(Q2\text{,}\) \(Q3\text{,}\) and \(Q4\) and proceed counterclockwise around the plane as shown in the graph at the left.

Where the two lines meet in the center is called the origin, marked by the green point. This center origin is where \(x = 0\) and \(y = 0\text{.}\)

Positive numbers appear to the right of the origin on the \(x\)-axis and negative numbers to the left. Similarly, positive numbers appear above the origin on the \(y\)-axis, and negative numbers below. We can put dots on the graph to indicate points. Each point has an "address" that defines its location. The address is given as an ordered pair \((x,y).\) The first number will be the value on the \(x\)-axis or horizontal number line. This is the distance from the point to the origin in the horizontal direction. The second number will represent the distance from the point to the origin in the vertical direction.

Example3.A.1Identify Quadrants and Coordinates

Give the quadrant and coordinates of each point.

The point \(A\) is in \(Q1\) and point \(B\) is in \(Q2\text{.}\) Point \(C\) is not in a quadrant but is on the \(y\)-axis.

Tracing from the origin, point \(A\) is right \(1\text{,}\) up \(4\text{.}\) This becomes \(A(1, 4)\text{.}\) Point \(B\) is left \(5\text{,}\) up \(3\text{.}\) Left is backwards or negative so we have \(B( - 5, 3)\text{.}\) \(C\) is straight down 2 units. There is no left or right. This means we go right zero so the point is \(C(0,-2)\text{.}\)

\(A(1,4)\) is in \(Q1\text{,}\) \(B(-5,3)\) is in \(Q2\text{,}\) and \(C(0,-2)\) is on the \(y\)-axis. \(\checkmark\)

WeBWorK: Entering Points

Points in the plane are entered as (#,#)= (horizontal #, vertical #).

Just as we can give the coordinates for a set of points, we can take a set of points and plot them on the plane.

Example3.A.2Graph Points

Graph the points

\(A(3, 2), B( - 2, 1), C(3, - 4), D( - 2, - 3), E( - 3, 0), F (0, 2), G(0, 0)\)

The first point, \(A\) is at \((3, 2)\text{.}\) This means \(x=3\)(right 3) and \(y =2\)(up 2). Following these instructions, starting from the origin, we get our point. Note that \(A\) is in Q1.

The second point, \(B( - 2, 1)\text{,}\) is left 2 (negative moves backwards), up 1. This is also illustrated on the graph. Note that \(B\) is in Q2.

The third point, \(C(3,-4)\) is right 3, down 4 (negative moves backwards). Note that \(C\) is in Q4.

The fourth point, \(D(-2,-3)\) is left 2, down 3 (both negative, both move backwards). Note that \(D\) is in Q3.

The last three points have zeros in them. We still treat these points just like the other points. If there is a zero there is just no movement.

Next is \(E(-3, 0)\text{.}\) This is left 3 (negative is backwards), and up zero, right on the \(x\)-axis. Note that \(E\) is on the \(x\)-axis.

Then is \(F(0, 2)\text{.}\) This is right zero, and up two, directly on the \(y\)-axis. Note that \(F\) on the \(y\)-axis.

Finally is \(G(0, 0)\text{.}\) This point has no movement. Thus the point is the origin.

Our Solution \(\checkmark\)

We will use graphs to give a picture of the solutions to an equation. To determine whether or not a point \((x,y)\) is a solution to a given equation, you substitute (or "plug in") the values into their corresponding variables in the equation and check to see that you get a true statement (both sides of the equation have equal values).

Example3.A.3Investigate Solution

Determine if the point \((1,-1)\) is a solution to the equation \(y = 2x - 3\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} y\amp=2x-3\amp\amp\text{Since \((1,-1)=(x,y)\)}\\ \amp\amp\amp\text{substitute \(x=1\) and \(y=-1\) in the equation.}\\ -1\amp=2(1)-3\amp\amp\text{Simplify}\\ -1\amp =-1\amp\amp\text{A true statement} \end{align*}

The point \((1,-1)\) is a solution.\(\checkmark\)

Example3.A.4Investigate Solution

Determine if the point \((-9,7)\) is a solution to the equation \(y = \lvert x+5\rvert+4\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} y\amp = \lvert x+5\rvert +4\amp\amp\text{Substitute \(x=-9\) and \(y=7\)}\\ \amp\amp\amp\text{in the equation.}\\ 7 \amp = \lvert -9+5\rvert +4\amp\amp\text{Simplify: Inside absolute value first}\\ 7\amp = \lvert -4\rvert +4\amp\amp\lvert -4\rvert =4\\ 7\amp =8\amp\amp\text{NOT a true statement: \(7\) does not equal \(8\)} \end{align*}

No, the point \((-9,7)\) is not a solution. \(\checkmark\)

Equations do not always use the varibles \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) but as long as we know which represents the horizontal distance and which the vertical, we may plot a graph to represent the equation.

Subsection3.A.2Intercepts

Intercepts are points on the graph which lie on an axis. Points on the \(y\)-axis will have an \(x\)-coordinate of \(0\text{.}\) Points on the \(x\)-axis will have a \(y\)-coordinate of \(0\text{.}\)

Example3.A.9Determine \(y\)-Intercepts: Set \(x=0\)

Determine the \(y\)-intercept of \(y=3x+2\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} y\amp =3x+2\amp\amp\text{ \(y\)-intercepts have an \(x\)-coordinate of \(0\): set \(x=0\)}\\ y\amp =3(0)+2\amp\amp\text{Solve for \(y\): Multiply \(3(0)\) first}\\ y\amp =0+2\amp\amp\text{Add}\\ y\amp =2\amp\amp\text{We have found the \(y\)-value of the \(y\)-intercept}\\ \amp (0,2)\amp\amp\text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}

The \(y\)-intercept \((0,2)\) is labeled on the graph of the equation.

Example3.A.10Determine \(x\)-Intercepts: Set \(y=0\)

Find the \(x\)-intercept of \(y=3x+2\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} y\amp =3x+2\amp\amp\text{ \(x\)-intercepts have a \(y\)-coordinate of \(0\): set \(y=0\)}\\ 0\amp =3x+2\amp\amp\text{Solve for \(x\): Subtract \(2\) from both sides}\\ -2\amp =3x\amp\amp\text{Divide both sides by \(3\)}\\ -\dfrac{2}{3}\amp =x\amp\amp\text{We have found the \(x\)-value of the \(x\)-intercept}\\ \amp\left(-\dfrac{2}{3}, 0\right)\amp\amp\text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}

The \(x\)-intercept \(\left(-\frac{2}{3} , 0\right)\) is labeled on the graph of the equation.

The intercepts are points where the graph crosses an axis. We may slightly abuse the notation and say "The \(x\)-intercept is \(-\frac{2}{3}\)" instead of referring to the point \(\left( -\frac{2}{3},0\right)\text{.}\)

When variables other than \(x\) and \(y\) are used in equations, we use the same process to calculate intercepts.

Example3.A.11Determine \(Q\)-Intercept

Find the \(Q\)-intercept of \(2a+5Q=8\text{.}\) In this case, let the vertical axis be given by \(Q\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} 2a+5Q\amp =8\amp\amp\text{ \(Q\)-intercepts have an \(a\)-coordinate of \(0\): set \(a=0\)}\\ 2(0)+5Q\amp =8\amp\amp\text{Simplify}\\ 5Q\amp =8\amp\amp\text{Divide both sides by \(5\)}\\ Q\amp =\dfrac{8}{5}\amp\amp\text{We have found the \(Q\)-value of the \(Q\)-intercept}\\ \amp\left(0, \dfrac{8}{5}\right)\amp\amp\text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}

The \(Q\)-intercept \(\left(0, \frac{8}{5}\right)\) is labeled on the graph of the equation.

Subsection3.A.3Equations and Graphs

There are situations where you have two (or more) equations and you need to know the values that "work" in both. In other words, what values are solutions to both problems described by the two equations? Alternatively, sometimes solving an equation can be thought of as finding common solutions to two equations.

Example3.A.12Points in Common

Identify the common point of the graphs shown below.

The common point is \((1,3).\)

We say the graphs intersect at common points.

The graphs intersect at \(t=1.\checkmark\)

Note: The location of the common point is often given by the value from the horizontal axis and not by the entire point.

Example3.A.13Investigate Equation Relationships Graphically

Consider the graph of \(y=\lvert x+1\rvert\) in red together with the line \(y=3\) in blue below. For what values of \(x\) does \(\lvert x+1\rvert =3\text{?}\)

Using the graph, we see the red \(y=\lvert x+1\rvert\) and blue line \(y=3\) intersect at the two points \((-4,3)\) and \((2,3)\text{.}\)

Thus, \(\lvert x+1\rvert =3\) at \(x=-4\) or \(x=2.\checkmark\)

Example3.A.14Investigate Equation Relationships Graphically

Consider the graph of \(y=\lvert x+1\rvert\) in red together with the line \(y=3\) in blue below. For what values of \(x\) is \(\lvert x+1\rvert \lt 3\text{?}\)

Using the graph, we see the red \(y=\lvert x+1\rvert\) lies below the blue line \(y=3\) for \(x\) values between \(-4\) and \(2\text{.}\)

Thus, \(\lvert x+1\rvert \lt 3\) for all \(x\) in the interval \((-4,2).\checkmark\)

Example3.A.15Investigate Equation Relationships Graphically

Consider the graph of \(y=\lvert x+1\rvert\) in red together with the line \(y=3\) in blue below. For what values of \(x\) is \(\lvert x+1\rvert \gt 3\text{?}\)

Using the graph, we see the red \(y=\lvert x+1\rvert\) lies above the blue line \(y=3\) for \(x\) values less than \(-4\) or above \(2\text{.}\)

Thus, \(\lvert x+1\rvert \gt 3\) for all \(x\) in the compound interval \((-\infty,-4)\cup (2,\infty).\checkmark\)
