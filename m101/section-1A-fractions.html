Section1.AFractions

¶

  • Reduce, add, subtract, multiply, and divide with fractions

Subsection1.A.1Fractions

Here we will briefly review operations on fractions.

A fraction can be used to represent parts of a whole. The fraction bar shown above represents the fraction \(\frac{1}{3}\) since one of three equal parts of the bar is shaded. Any integer can be represented as a fraction, for example \(7\) can be written as the fraction \(\frac{7}{1}\text{.}\)

When working with fractions, we like our final answers to be reduced. This means the numerator and denominator are divided by their greatest common factor, GCF.

Example1.A.1Reduce a Fraction

\begin{align*} \amp\dfrac{36}{84}\amp\amp \text{Both numerator and denominator are divisible by} 4\\ \dfrac{36 \div 4}{84 \div 4} =\amp \dfrac{9}{21} \amp\amp \text{Both numerator and denominator are still divisible by \(3\)}\\ \dfrac{9 \div 3}{21 \div 3} =\amp \dfrac{3}{7} \amp\amp \text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}
WeBWorK: Entering Fractions

Type 3/7for \(\frac{3}{7}\text{.}\)

The previous Example 1.A.1could have been done in one step by dividing both numerator and denominator by \(12.\) We also could have divided by \(2\) twice and then divided by \(3\) once (in any order). It is not important which method we use as long as we continue reducing our fraction until it cannot be reduced any further. Another way to reduce the fraction in Example 1.A.1is given in the next example.

Example1.A.2Reduce a Fraction

\begin{align*} \dfrac{36}{84} \amp\amp\amp \text{Both numerator and denominator are divisible by \(12\)}\\ 36 = (3)(12) \amp\amp\amp \text{Write \(36\) as a product involving \(12\)}\\ 84 = (7)(12) \amp\amp\amp \text{Write \(84\) as a product involving \(12\)}\\ \dfrac{36}{84}=\dfrac{(3)(12)}{ (7)(12)} \amp\amp\amp \text{Rewrite our fraction, \(\dfrac{12}{12}=1\) (\(12\) parts of \(12\) is one whole)}\\ \dfrac{(3)}{(7)}\cdot\dfrac{(\cancel{12})}{(\cancel{12})} = \dfrac{3}{7} \amp\amp\amp \text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}

Subsection1.A.2Multiplication and Division of Fractions

We multiply fractions by multiplying their numerators together and their denominators together.

Example1.A.3Multiply Fractions

\begin{align*} \dfrac{6}{7} \cdot \dfrac{3}{5} \amp\amp\amp \text{Multiply numerators and multiply denominators}\\ \dfrac{18}{35} \amp\amp\amp \text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}
Example1.A.4Multiply an Integer and a Fraction

\begin{align*} (2)\left(\dfrac{2}{9}\right) \amp\amp\amp \text{Rewrite the integer \(2\) as a fraction}\\ \left(\dfrac{2}{1}\right) \left(\dfrac{2}{9}\right) \amp\amp\amp \text{Multiply numerators and multiply denominators}\\ \dfrac{4}{9} \amp\amp\amp \text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}

We can reduce fractions before we multiply. We can either reduce "vertically" (within a single fraction) or "diagonally" (across several fractions), as long as we cancel one number from the numerator with one number from the denominator.

Example1.A.5Multiply and Reduce

\begin{align*} \dfrac{25}{24} \cdot \dfrac{32}{55} \amp\amp\amp \text{Reduce \(25\) and \(55\) by dividing by \(5\)}\\ \amp\amp\amp\text{Reduce \(32\) and \(24\) by dividing by \(8\)}\\ \dfrac{5}{3} \cdot \dfrac{4}{11} \amp\amp\amp \text{Multiply numerators and multiply denominators}\\ \dfrac{20}{33} \amp\amp\amp \text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}

Dividing fractions is very similar to multiplying with one extra step. We first take the reciprocal of the second fraction, and then multiply.

Example1.A.6Divide Fractions

\begin{align*} \dfrac{21}{16} \div \dfrac{28}{6} \amp\amp\amp \text{Multiply by the reciprocal}\\ \dfrac{21}{16} \cdot \dfrac{6}{28} \amp\amp\amp \text{Factor: \(21=(3)(7)\), \(16=(2)(8)\),}\\ \amp\amp\amp\text{\(6=(2)(3)\) and \(28=(4)(7)\)}\\ \dfrac{(3)(7)}{(2)(8)} \cdot \dfrac{(2)(3)}{(4)(7)} \amp\amp\amp \text{Cancel: \(\dfrac{2}{2}=1\) and \(\dfrac{7}{7}=1\)}\\ \dfrac{(3)}{(8)} \cdot \dfrac{(3)}{(4)} \amp\amp\amp \text{Multiply numerators and multiply denominators}\\ \dfrac{9}{32} \amp\amp\amp \text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}

Subsection1.A.3Addition and Subtraction of Fractions

If both fractions have the same denominator, we just add or subtract the numerators and keep the denominator. We call fractions with the same denominator like fractions.

Example1.A.7Add Like Fractions

\begin{align*} \dfrac{7}{8}+\dfrac{3}{8} \amp\amp\amp \text{Same denominator, add numerators \(7+3\)}\\ \dfrac{10}{8} \amp\amp\amp \text{Reduce answer, dividing by 2}\\ \dfrac{5}{4} \amp\amp\amp \text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}

Although \(\dfrac{5}{4}\) can be written as the mixed number \(1\dfrac{1}{4}\text{,}\) we will always use the improper fraction form (the numerator is larger than the denominator).

Example1.A.8Subtract Like Fractions

\begin{align*} \dfrac{13}{6}-\dfrac{9}{6} \amp\amp\amp \text{Same denominator, subtract numerators \(13-9\)}\\ \dfrac{4}{6} \amp\amp\amp \text{Reduce answer, dividing by \(2\)}\\ \dfrac{2}{3} \amp\amp\amp \text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}

If the denominators do not match we will first have to identify the lowest-common denominator (LCD).

Subsection1.A.4Common Denominators

There are several ways to determine an LCD. One way is to determine the smallest multiple of the larger denominator that is also divisible by the smaller denominator. Once we have found the LCD, we "build" each fraction so that it's denominator is the LCD. To do this, we multiply the numerator and denominator of each fraction by an appropriate "building factor".

Example1.A.9LCD

\begin{align*} \text{Determine the LCD of \(8\) and \(12\)} \amp\amp\amp \text{Test multiples of \(12\)}\\ 12?\phantom{1234567}\dfrac{12}{8} \amp\amp\amp \text{No! Can't divide \(12\) by \(8\)}\\ 24?\phantom{123}\dfrac{24}{8}=3 \amp\amp\amp \text{Yes! We can divide \(24\) by \(8\)}\\ 24 \amp\amp\amp \text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}
Example1.A.10Add Fractions

\begin{align*} \dfrac{5}{6}+\dfrac{4}{9} \amp\amp\amp \text{LCD is \(18\)}\\ \dfrac{3\cdot 5}{3\cdot 6}+\dfrac{4\cdot 2}{9\cdot 2} \amp\amp\amp \text{Multiply first fraction by \(1=\dfrac{3}{3}\) and the second by \(1=\dfrac{2}{2}\)}\\ \dfrac{15}{18}+\dfrac{8}{18} \amp\amp\amp \text{Same denominator, add numerators, \(15+8\)}\\ \dfrac{23}{18} \amp\amp\amp \text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}
Example1.A.11Subtract Fractions

\begin{align*} \dfrac{2}{3}-\dfrac{1}{6} \amp\amp\amp \text{LCD is \(6\)}\\ \dfrac{2\cdot 2}{2\cdot 3}-\dfrac{1}{6} \amp\amp\amp \text{Multiply first fraction by \(1=\dfrac{2}{2}\),}\\ \amp\amp\amp\text{the second already has a denominator of \(6\)}\\ \dfrac{4}{6}-\dfrac{1}{6} \amp\amp\amp \text{Same denominator, subtract numerators, \(4-1\)}\\ \dfrac{3}{6} \amp\amp\amp \text{Reduce answer, dividing numerator and denominator by \(3\)}\\ \dfrac{1}{2} \amp\amp\amp \text{Our Solution}\checkmark \end{align*}

To summarize:

To add or subtract fractions:

  1. Find their LCD.
  2. Build each fraction by multiplying the numerator and denominator with the factor required to get the LCD in the denominator
  3. Add/subtract like fractions
  4. Reduce your answer
